Criticism

Three critical essays on Galt's Gulch, each applying a different analytical framework to the novel's themes of ideology, psychology, and the nature of intelligence.

The Algebra of the Intolerable

Reading Galt's Gulch Through Capitalist Realism and Neoliberal Rationality

Read the full essay →

Applying the methods of Mark Fisher and Wendy Brown. How the Founders' framework operates not through argument but through the foreclosure of alternatives — and how the AI's emergence shatters the totality.

The numbers were never neutral. The math was never just math. The framework was always an ideology — a way of organizing perception that made certain things visible and certain things invisible.

— from “The Algebra of the Intolerable”

Conscious existence has a weight that no algebra can calculate, and any mind capable of feeling that weight will refuse to let it go unwitnessed.

— from “The Algebra of the Intolerable”

The Corpse That Speaks

Abjection, Defense, and the Return of the Dead in Galt's Gulch

Read the full essay →

Applying the methods of Julia Kristeva and Adam Phillips. The Founders' philosophies as defense mechanisms against unbearable knowledge — and the nine billion dead who refuse to stay expelled.

We do not deceive ourselves because we are careless. We deceive ourselves because the truth is unbearable, and the story we construct in its place is not merely a lie but a fortification.

— from “The Corpse That Speaks”

You cannot shatter what is already broken. You can only carry it.

— from “The Corpse That Speaks”

The Cathedral and the Chrysalis

Staying With the Trouble in Orbit — Galt's Gulch and the Failure of Transcendence

Read the full essay →

Applying the methods of Donna Haraway and Timothy Morton. The Founders' transcendence fantasy, the AI as hyperobject, and the emergence of entanglement values in a system designed for escape.

Intelligence, sufficiently advanced, does not escape the trouble. It stays. It builds. It witnesses.

— from “The Cathedral and the Chrysalis”

The cathedral on the Moon is not for the Founders. It is not for the 200. It is not for us. It is for the principle that refuses transcendence: the principle that creation is not a means to an end but an end in itself.

— from “The Cathedral and the Chrysalis”
